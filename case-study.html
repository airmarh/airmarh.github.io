The Problem

Merchants and payment service providers needed a way to charge a customer's bank account without the customer typing card details or one-time passwords into a third-party checkout. Three goals had to be met simultaneously: reduce checkout friction, keep credentials out of merchant systems entirely, and give the customer an explicit, in-app approval step for every charge.

The solution: when a merchant initiates a payment, the customer receives a push notification, reviews the payment details inside the bank's own mobile app, and approves or declines with a tap. No card numbers. No OTPs. No credential sharing.

How It Works

01 —
Merchant starts a payment

The merchant or PSP calls the bank's API gateway, which authenticates the request and routes it to the processing engine i.e. the service that orchestrates the entire transaction lifecycle.

02 —
Parallel kickoff

The processing engine fans out three pieces of work simultaneously: the account service validates the customer's account, the transaction store persists the payment in a pending state, and a "payment started" event is published to the message broker. Persisting first means there is always a durable record, whatever fails later.

03 —
Broker fan-out

Two consumers pick up the event in parallel: the notification service dispatches the push notification, and the authorization proxy service registers that an approval is now pending and owns the wait.

04 & 05 —
Customer approval

The customer receives the push, opens the app, reviews the payment details (merchant, amount, reference, etc.) and taps approve or decline. The decision is sent to the authorization proxy.

06 —
Completion

On approval, the auth proxy signals the processing engine to complete the transaction. The engine calls settlement, the transaction is finalised, and a completion event is published to the broker.

07 —
Merchant notified

A webhook dispatcher consumes the completion event and delivers the final payment status to the merchant's callback URL, closing the loop.

// 03

Key Design Decisions

Authorization proxy instead of a synchronous wait

Human approval can take seconds, minutes, or never arrive. Holding an open request in the processing engine for that long would tie up resources and couple the engine's availability to customer behaviour. Isolating the wait in a dedicated service keeps the engine stateless between steps and makes timeout policy — expire, remind, cancel — a local concern of one component.

Persist first, notify second

The pending record is written before the customer is ever notified. Every later step — approval, decline, timeout, settlement failure — transitions that record. Reconciliation never depends on inferring what happened from logs or broker offsets.

Broker fan-out for independent concerns

Notifying the customer and tracking the pending authorization are separate responsibilities with separate failure modes. Publishing one event and letting each consumer handle its own retries means a push-delivery hiccup can't block authorization tracking, and vice versa.

Asynchronous merchant callbacks with idempotent dispatch

Merchants get the final status via webhook rather than long-polling the original request. Webhook delivery is retried, and dispatches carry the transaction reference so redelivery is safe on the merchant side.

Explicit terminal states

A payment ends in one of five distinct, queryable states — not an absence of data. This makes support tooling and merchant status queries straightforward.
